Down Payments

How much do you need upfront?

Your minimum down payment in Canada depends on the purchase price of the home. Alberta follows federal rules — and has one major advantage: no provincial land transfer tax, saving buyers thousands compared to other provinces.

Putting down 20% or more means no CMHC insurance is required — giving you more options and typically lower effective borrowing costs.

Purchase Price Min. Down Payment CMHC Required?
Under $500,000 5% Yes
$500K – $999,999 5% on first $500K + 10% on remainder Yes
$1M – $1.49M 20% No
$1.5M+ 20% minimum No
CMHC Insurance

What is CMHC insurance?

If your down payment is less than 20%, your mortgage must be insured through CMHC (or Sagen / Canada Guaranty). The premium is added to your mortgage and paid off monthly. The benefit: insured mortgages often carry lower interest rates because lenders take on less risk.

Down Payment LTV Ratio Premium Rate
5% 95% 4.00%
10% 90% 3.10%
15% 85% 2.80%
20%+ 80% or less None
Example: On a $500,000 home with 5% down ($25,000), your mortgage is $475,000. The CMHC premium (4%) adds $19,000 — bringing your insured mortgage to $494,000. This premium is paid over your amortization period, not upfront.
Mortgage Stress Test

What is the stress test?

The federal mortgage stress test ensures you can still afford your mortgage if interest rates rise. It applies to all federally regulated lenders — whether your down payment is 5% or 25%.

You must qualify at the higher of: your contract rate + 2%, or the benchmark rate of 5.25% — whichever is greater. Good news: switching lenders at renewal (without refinancing) no longer requires a stress test re-qualification.

1
Find your qualifying rate
Take your actual contract rate and add 2%. Compare that to 5.25% — whichever is higher is your qualifying rate.
2
GDS & TDS ratios checked
Your Gross Debt Service (housing costs / income) should be under 39%. Total Debt Service (all debts / income) should be under 44%.
3
Credit score minimum
A minimum credit score of 680 is required to qualify for CMHC-insured mortgages. Conventional lenders may accept lower scores with 20%+ down.
Fixed vs Variable

Which rate type is right for you?

There's no universal answer — the right choice depends on your financial situation, risk tolerance, and how long you plan to stay in the property. Here's what each option offers:

Fixed Rate
Stability & Certainty
  • Payment stays the same throughout the term
  • Easier to budget and plan
  • Typically higher rate than variable
  • Best for risk-averse buyers
  • Ideal in rising rate environments
Variable Rate
Flexibility & Potential Savings
  • Rate moves with Bank of Canada prime
  • Often lower to start
  • Payment may fluctuate
  • Lower break penalty if needed
  • Best when rates are declining
Broker's note: As of early 2026, the Bank of Canada rate cycle has shifted. Talk to a mortgage professional before locking in — the right term length matters as much as fixed vs. variable.
First-Time Buyer Programs

Programs that can save you thousands

Alberta doesn't have a provincial land transfer tax — already a major advantage over Ontario or BC. On top of that, several federal programs are available to first-time buyers that can meaningfully reduce your upfront costs.

Up to $40,000
First Home Savings Account (FHSA)
A tax-advantaged account combining RRSP and TFSA benefits. Contributions are tax-deductible and withdrawals for a qualifying home purchase are completely tax-free.
Up to $60,000 / person
Home Buyers' Plan (HBP)
Withdraw up to $60,000 ($120,000 per couple) tax-free from your RRSP to fund your down payment. Repayable over 15 years. Can be combined with the FHSA.
Up to $1,500 back
First-Time Home Buyers' Tax Credit
A non-refundable federal tax credit on up to $10,000 of your qualifying home purchase. Simple to claim on your tax return — recovers up to $1,500 in the year you buy.
5% rebate
GST Relief on New Builds
First-time buyers purchasing new or substantially renovated homes up to $1M may qualify for a full GST rebate — saving up to $50,000 on eligible new construction purchases.
Lower monthly payments
30-Year Amortization
First-time buyers (and all buyers of new builds) can now access 30-year insured amortizations — reducing monthly payments and improving qualification flexibility.
$2,000 down
Attainable Homes Calgary
A Calgary-specific program that helps buyers purchase with just $2,000 down. AHC loans the rest of the down payment interest-free, in exchange for a share of appreciation.

Alberta's Hidden Advantage: No Land Transfer Tax

Unlike Ontario or BC — where land transfer taxes can exceed $15,000 on a $700K home — Alberta charges only minor land title registration fees (typically $50–$200). This means more of your money goes toward your home, not government fees. It's one of the most buyer-friendly provinces in Canada for closing costs.

Pre-Approval Process

Why get pre-approved first?

Getting pre-approved before you start house hunting isn't just smart — it's essential in a competitive Calgary market. Pre-approval tells you exactly what you can afford, locks in your rate for up to 120 days, and signals to sellers that you're a serious buyer.

Here's what lenders will review when assessing your application:

Proof of income (T4s, NOAs, pay stubs)
Credit score (minimum 680 for insured mortgages)
Down payment source and documentation
Employment history (2+ years preferred)
Existing debts and monthly obligations
GDS / TDS ratio assessment
Refinancing & Renewals

Already in a mortgage? Here's what to know.

Whether your term is coming up or you're looking to access equity, there are several options available to existing homeowners in Alberta.

Mortgage Renewal
When your term ends, you renegotiate your rate and terms. Since 2024, switching lenders at renewal no longer requires passing the stress test — giving you more freedom to shop around.
Refinancing
Access your home's equity before your term ends — for renovations, debt consolidation, or investment. Refinancing does require the stress test and may involve a break penalty.
HELOC
A Home Equity Line of Credit lets you borrow against your equity on a revolving basis, up to 80% of your home's value. Flexible draws and repayments — interest only on what you use.
Investment Properties
Investment properties require a minimum 20% down from your own savings — no gifted or borrowed funds allowed. Rental income can factor into qualification calculations.
